# The Story of the AI-* Family

## How to Train Your AI — An Enterprise Story

### Version 3: The Short Flight

---

### I. The Village and Its Dragons

There is a village where hundreds of builders make enormous things — systems that serve millions, platforms that run businesses. Every new project starts the same way: chaos. Twenty teams in twenty directions. Architects arguing. New recruits lost.

And circling overhead: dragons. Powerful. Unpredictable. The village calls them AI.

Some tried to use them. Most got burned. The dragons would build walls in wrong places, write things nobody asked for, generate confident nonsense with a smile. So the village did what it does with things it fears — it fought them. Policies. Restrictions. *"Not ready for enterprise."*

But one person saw differently. Not a slayer — a *rider*. Someone who noticed the problem was never the dragon's power. It was the absence of a saddle.

> *What if you could teach a dragon to build an enterprise?*

---

### II. Ten Dragons, One Flock

The rider didn't train one dragon. Over time — through patience, failure, and hard-won lessons — they trained *ten*. Each for a different challenge. Each with a different temperament. Each needing a different rider.

Some catch sparks. Some design blueprints. Some guard what's been built. Some demand proof. One doesn't even belong to the village — a wild dragon from the outside that the trained ones learned to fly beside.

Together, they became a flock:

| Dragon | What It Does |
|--------|-------------|
| ✨ **AI-ILC** | Catches sparks. Decides what's worth building. |
| 🔵 **AI-PILC** | Forges governance. Turns ideas into chartered projects. |
| 🦅 **AI-PPM** | Flies above. Governs the portfolio of all projects. |
| ⚡ **AI-FLO** | Routes signals. The nervous system between dragons. |
| 🟣 **AI-ADLC** | Architects. Decomposes systems, records why. |
| 🎨 **AI-UXD** | Speaks for users. Maps journeys and experience. |
| 🏆 **AI-POLC** | Owns the product. Prioritizes and steers. |
| 🟢 **AI-DWG** | Bridges the gap. Prepares the workspace. |
| 🌑 **AI-GCE** | Guards the rules. Watches from the shadows. |
| 🔬 **AI-TGE** | Demands proof. Governs what must be tested. |
| ☀️ **AI-DLC** ¹ | Builds. The wild dragon we trained the others to feed. |

> ¹ AI-DLC is Amazon's open-source build lifecycle — not ours. Our flock produces what it consumes.

---

### III. The Two Skies

The village discovered something as the flock grew: not all dragons fly at the same height.

Some look *across* — at many projects, their conflicts, their priorities. These fly the high sky.

Some look *down* — at a single project, its code, its tests, its users. These fly the low sky.

And one flies *between* — carrying signals from high to low and back again.

**The High Sky (Portfolio Layer):**
AI-ILC catches the idea → AI-PILC charters the project → AI-PPM governs the portfolio.

**The Edge (Router):**
AI-FLO routes outputs to the right next dragon. The nervous system.

**The Low Sky (Project Layer):**
AI-ADLC + AI-UXD + AI-POLC work in parallel (design, experience, ownership) → All feed AI-DWG (prepare the workspace) → AI-DLC builds, while AI-GCE guards and AI-TGE proves → AI-POLC steers delivery throughout.

The high sky decides *what* to build and *whether* it's worth it. The low sky decides *how* to build it and ensures it's built *right*. The router ensures nothing falls between them.

---

### IV. A Day in the Village

Let us tell you what changed.

A junior developer joins the team. In the old village, they'd spend six weeks reading wikis nobody maintained, asking questions nobody had time to answer, writing code that violated conventions nobody told them about.

Now? They open their workspace. The rules are *alive* — steering their AI assistant, guiding every line toward the architecture someone decided months ago. The dark dragon watches. The proving dragon tracks. The product dragon has already prioritized their first task. They contribute *on day one*.

Or: someone has an idea in a meeting. In the old village, that idea would evaporate by Friday. Now? The spark dragon catches it. Evaluates it. Routes it. If it's worth a project, the initiation dragon forges governance. If the portfolio dragon approves, the design dragons begin. Weeks of manual handoffs — gone. Replaced by a flock that carries context like birds carry seeds.

Or: architecture changes. In the old village, the updated document sits in a repo nobody checks. The codebase drifts. Tests go stale. Now? The change cascades. Through the workspace dragon into updated rules. Through the guardian into updated enforcement. Through the proving dragon into updated test requirements. A decision made at 10 AM is governing code by noon.

---

### V. Riders, Not Passengers

Every dragon has a rider. This matters.

The innovation manager rides the spark-catcher. The CTO rides the architect. The product owner rides the backlog. The QA lead rides the prover. None of them are passengers. None of them hand the reins to the dragon and hope for the best.

The dragons don't replace expertise. They *extend* it. A product owner with AI-POLC still makes every prioritization decision — but now those decisions are structured, traceable, and remembered. A CTO with AI-ADLC still chooses the architecture — but now that choice flows into every line of code, every test, every enforcement rule. Automatically. Without decay.

> The partnership isn't "AI does the work." It's **"AI holds the structure while the human makes the decisions."**

That's what a saddle does. It doesn't make the dragon less powerful. It makes the rider's intent *matter*.

---

### VI. The Real Enemy

This was never about dragons. It was about *chaos*.

Enterprise software has always had the same enemy: complexity at scale. Teams that don't talk. Architecture that lives in one head. Standards in wikis nobody reads. Decisions that die between meetings and codebases. Products without clear owners. Tests without strategy. Portfolios without visibility.

The dragons didn't create that chaos. And fighting the dragons didn't fix it. What fixed it was *training* them — giving each one a purpose, a boundary, a rider, and a place in the formation.

> *The dragons were never the enemy. Unstructured chaos was the enemy. The dragons are how you defeat it.*

---

### VII. The Invitation

Some of these dragons fly today. Some are stretching their wings. Some are being forged in fire and patience.

But the village has changed. It no longer fears the dragons. It *breeds* them. Purpose-built. Trained. Governed — because an ungoverned dragon is still dangerous.

What happens when the gap between "someone had an idea" and "tested, governed software in production" is no longer measured in quarters — but in days?

That's the story we're writing.

And we're not done.
